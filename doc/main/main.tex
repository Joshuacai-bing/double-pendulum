% Abstract
\begin{abstract}

This study examines the theoretical and simulated behavior of a double pendulum confined to small angular displacements ($\le$ 0.15 rad) with linear viscous damping. Using a Lagrangian formulation, the linearised equations of motion are derived and the undamped normal mode frequencies are obtained analytically. A single‑pendulum experiment provides an empirical estimate of the local damping coefficient, which is then incorporated into the double‑pendulum model. Numerical simulations are performed using a symplectic integrator (Gauss‑Legendre) that preserves energy in the conservative limit and correctly reproduces exponential decay when damping is added. The simulated angular displacements, phase portraits, and frequency spectra agree quantitatively with theoretical predictions: the two natural frequencies (approximately 0.38 Hz and 0.92 Hz for equal masses and lengths) are recovered with a deviation below 0.3\%. A parameter sweep over the mass ratio \(m_2/m_1\) from 0.1 to 5.0 reveals how kinetic energy partitions between the upper and lower segments, with the lower pendulum dominating the energy for mass ratios near unity. The damped simulations show inward‑spiralling phase trajectories and a rapid exponential decay of mechanical energy, consistent with the experimentally estimated damping coefficient. The results demonstrate that linearised theory, combined with empirically identified damping, provides a quantitatively reliable baseline for weakly excited double pendulums, although fully nonlinear effects require larger amplitudes or external forcing.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Classical mechanics is deterministic in form, yet many deterministic systems are far from simple in practice. The double pendulum stands as a classic paradigm of this tension. Comprising two point masses connected by rigid rods, its governing equations are mathematically exact, yet its resulting motion often appears utterly irregular over finite observation windows. Small differences in initial conditions can produce strongly divergent trajectories, a hallmark of non-linear dynamics. What exactly happens when the neat scaffolding of theory meets physical complexity? This contrast motivates the present investigation.

In the small-angle limit, the system’s behavior is easily wrangled by linear normal-mode theory, yielding predictable, synchronous oscillations. Push it outside that limit, however, and trigonometric non-linearities—manifesting as Coriolis and centrifugal forces—unleash far richer, chaotic responses. We are not focusing on chaos as some isolated curiosity here. Rather, this study centers on the broad dynamic spectrum of the double pendulum, specifically exploring how varying system parameters (such as mass ratios) and initial phase conditions shift the system between predictable regular states and complex energetic exchanges. In practice, of course, the reality is messier when energy dissipation is introduced.

This naturally leads to the question of how accurately small-angle analytical predictions actually describe the system when subjected to realistic, non-conservative forces. A related, though less discussed, point concerns the inclusion of experimentally identified damping mechanisms: how do they reshape the global phase-space geometry and modulate the continuous transfer of kinetic energy between the two masses? These inquiries anchor the connection between analytical mechanics, advanced numerical modelling, and empirical data.

Methodologically, this work weaves theory with high-precision simulation and physical experiment. A Lagrangian formulation yields the exact non-linear equations of motion, followed closely by linearization to extract small-angle modal frequencies and eigenvector amplitude ratios as a reference baseline. But analytical solutions only take us so far. Because the system is fundamentally non-separable and prone to artificial energy drift, we rely on a fully implicit symplectic integrator—the Gauss-Legendre Runge-Kutta scheme—to perform robust, purely data-driven numerical simulations. To capture the full spectrum of energy dissipation, our computational model is explicitly augmented with both linear viscous damping at the joints and quadratic air resistance proportional to the square of the angular velocities. By sweeping across varying mass distributions under specific modal excitations (in-phase, anti-phase, and mixed-phase), we meticulously map the partitioning of kinetic energy. Alongside this, a single-pendulum experiment grounds the model by providing empirically estimated local damping coefficients, which are subsequently fed back into these highly coupled, non-linear equations to visualize the kinematic decay and phase-space collapse.

Ultimately, by evaluating the system’s response through time-domain series, energy distribution plots, complex phase-space trajectories, and Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) spectral signatures, we aim to quantitatively validate the linear approximation while critically assessing the profound non-linear interactions that dominate the physical system's transient dynamics.

\section{Literature Review}

Formulations based on the Lagrangian and Hamiltonian frameworks \cite{goldstein2002classical} provide the mathematical basis for deriving the equations of motion for coupled oscillators. Historically, analytical efforts concentrated on the small-angle approximation, where the system's governing equations are linearized. This linearization reveals the normal modes of the system—specifically, the in-phase and anti-phase synchronous oscillations—allowing for closed-form solutions that define the baseline frequencies \cite{landau1976mechanics}. 

The majority of contemporary literature shifts focus rapidly toward the large-angle, chaotic regime, treating the linear double pendulum merely as a pedagogical stepping stone \cite{shinbrot1992chaos}. However, the strictly linear or weakly non-linear dynamics of the double pendulum possess a rich parametric structure that is often overshadowed by discussions of deterministic chaos. Specifically, the mass ratio between the upper and lower pendulum segments acts as a primary determinant of dynamic coupling strength \cite{ohlhoff2000forces}. Studies investigating energy partitioning in the linear regime reveal that varying this mass distribution drastically alters the relative amplitudes of oscillation between the two masses. This parametric tuning creates distinct regimes of energy localization, where kinetic energy can be biased heavily toward the upper or lower segment, a phenomenon critical for designing tuned mass dampers \cite{strogatz2018nonlinear}. A quantitative mapping of this energy distribution as a continuous function of mass ratio under specific initial phase excitations remains an area ripe for systematic exploration.

To analyze the governing equations beyond purely analytical solutions, the trajectory of double pendulum research has advanced alongside numerical integration techniques. Standard explicit solvers, such as the classical fourth-order Runge-Kutta method, are frequently utilized for mapping short-term transient dynamics. To combat artificial energy drift in longer simulations of conservative systems, computational researchers rely on symplectic integrators. The application of implicit methods, notably the Gauss-Legendre Runge-Kutta schemes, is vital for maintaining bounded energy error over arbitrarily long integrations \cite{hairer2006geometric}. 

Transitioning from idealized conservative models to dissipative systems, the introduction of velocity-dependent forces transforms the governing equations. In the linear regime, damping mechanisms dictate the time evolution toward rest attractors, fundamentally altering the decay envelopes of the normal modes \cite{baker1996pendulum}. Empirical studies emphasize the extraction of damping coefficients directly from experimental data, such as single-pendulum logarithmic decrement curves, to ground these numerical models \cite{peters1999resonance}. While this synthesis of parameter identification with numerical simulation is well-established, conflicting findings persist regarding whether linear viscous damping or quadratic air drag dominates the specific modal decay rates in macroscopic setups. This highlights the ongoing need for numerical models that explicitly integrate both linear and quadratic dissipative mechanisms simultaneously to match empirical energy dissipation curves accurately.

\section{Theoretical Analysis}
To thoroughly investigate the dynamics of the double pendulum, a robust mathematical model must be established. This section derives the equations of motion using Lagrangian mechanics, applies small-angle approximations to find analytical solutions, and explores the physical implications of the system's normal modes.

\subsection{Kinematics and the Lagrangian}
Consider a double pendulum consisting of two point masses, $m_1$ and $m_2$, suspended by two massless rigid rods of lengths $l_1$ and $l_2$. Let $\theta_1$ and $\theta_2$ be the angles the rods make with the vertical downward direction. The Cartesian coordinates of the masses, taking the suspension point as the origin and the downward direction as the positive $y$-axis, are given by:
\begin{align*}
x_1 &= l_1 \sin \theta_1, \quad y_1 = l_1 \cos \theta_1, \\
x_2 &= l_1 \sin \theta_1 + l_2 \sin \theta_2, \quad y_2 = l_1 \cos \theta_1 + l_2 \cos \theta_2.
\end{align*}

The kinetic energy ($T$) of the system is the sum of the kinetic energies of both masses, and the potential energy ($V$) is determined by their vertical positions in a uniform gravitational field $g$:
\begin{align*}
T &= \frac{1}{2}m_1(\dot{x}_1^2 + \dot{y}_1^2) + \frac{1}{2}m_2(\dot{x}_2^2 + \dot{y}_2^2), \\
V &= -m_1 g y_1 - m_2 g y_2.
\end{align*}

By substituting the time derivatives of the coordinates into the kinetic energy equation, the Lagrangian of the system, defined as $L = T - V$, is constructed:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
L &= \frac{1}{2}(m_1 + m_2)l_1^2\dot{\theta}_1^2 + \frac{1}{2}m_2 l_2^2 \dot{\theta}_2^2 + m_2 l_1 l_2 \dot{\theta}_1 \dot{\theta}_2 \cos(\theta_1 - \theta_2) \\
  &\quad + (m_1 + m_2)g l_1 \cos\theta_1 + m_2 g l_2 \cos\theta_2.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Non-linear Coupling, Fictitious Forces, and Symplectic Integration}
A critical feature of the Lagrangian derived above is the kinetic energy coupling term, $m_2 l_1 l_2 \dot{\theta}_1 \dot{\theta}_2 \cos(\theta_1 - \theta_2)$. This term indicates that the kinetic energy depends not only on the generalized velocities ($\dot{\theta}_1, \dot{\theta}_2$) but also on the generalized coordinates (specifically the angle difference $\theta_1 - \theta_2$). Consequently, the mass matrix of the system is configuration-dependent, making the system fundamentally non-separable.

When applying the Euler-Lagrange equations, the derivatives of this coupling term naturally give rise to terms that physically represent fictitious forces in the non-inertial rotational reference frame. Taking the partial derivative of the Lagrangian with respect to the position coordinate, $\frac{\partial L}{\partial \theta_1}$, produces terms proportional to the square of the angular velocities (e.g., $\dot{\theta}_2^2$). Physically, this represents the centrifugal force, an outward inertial pull exerted by the rotation of one pendulum upon the other. A related point concerns the Coriolis force, which emerges when taking the time derivative of the generalized momentum, $\frac{d}{dt}\left(\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{\theta}_1}\right)$. This operation requires applying the chain rule to the $\cos(\theta_1 - \theta_2)$ term, generating cross-product velocity terms (e.g., $\dot{\theta}_1 \dot{\theta}_2$). These terms correspond to a lateral deflection force arising when the relative angle between the two swinging pendulums changes.

But these ``forces'' are not external physical interactions; they are purely geometric consequences of applying analytical mechanics within a rotating coordinate system, even if they feel quite real in practice. If the full non-linear equations of motion are expressed in the matrix form $M(\mathbf{\theta})\ddot{\mathbf{\theta}} + C(\mathbf{\theta}, \dot{\mathbf{\theta}})\dot{\mathbf{\theta}} + G(\mathbf{\theta}) = 0$, the matrix $C$ is explicitly recognized as the Coriolis and centrifugal force matrix.

Furthermore, the non-separable nature of the system has profound implications for numerical simulations. Because the kinetic energy is coordinate-dependent, standard explicit symplectic integrators (such as the semi-implicit Euler or velocity Verlet algorithms) cannot perfectly preserve symplecticity, inevitably leading to energy drift over long integration periods. To guarantee strict energy conservation and maintain high precision in such non-separable systems, fully implicit symplectic algorithms, such as the Gauss-Legendre Runge-Kutta methods, must be employed.

\subsection{Equations of Motion and Small-Angle Approximation}
The dynamics of the system are governed by the Euler-Lagrange equations:
\begin{align*}
\frac{d}{dt}\left(\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{\theta}_i}\right) - \frac{\partial L}{\partial \theta_i} &= 0, \quad \text{for } i = 1, 2.
\end{align*}

Evaluating these partial derivatives yields a set of highly non-linear, coupled second-order differential equations. For arbitrary angles, these equations exhibit chaotic behavior and can only be solved numerically. However, to understand the fundamental oscillatory properties, we analyze the system under the small-angle approximation, where $\theta_1, \theta_2 \ll 1$. Applying Taylor series expansions, we approximate $\sin\theta \approx \theta$ and $\cos\theta \approx 1 - \frac{\theta^2}{2}$, while ignoring higher-order terms such as $\dot{\theta}^2\theta$. The linearized equations of motion become:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
(m_1 + m_2)l_1 \ddot{\theta}_1 + m_2 l_2 \ddot{\theta}_2 + (m_1 + m_2)g \theta_1 &= 0, \\
m_2 l_1 \ddot{\theta}_1 + m_2 l_2 \ddot{\theta}_2 + m_2 g \theta_2 &= 0.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Derivation of Natural Frequencies}
The full double-pendulum equations are non-linear, so the angular frequencies discussed in this subsection are not exact global frequencies of the original system. Instead, they are obtained by first linearizing the non-linear equations under the small-angle approximation and then solving the resulting linear eigenvalue problem. In this linear regime, we seek oscillatory solutions of the form $\theta_1(t) = A_1 e^{i\omega t}$ and $\theta_2(t) = A_2 e^{i\omega t}$, where $\omega$ is the angular frequency and $A_1, A_2$ are the amplitudes. Substituting these into the linearized equations yields:
\begin{equation}
\begin{pmatrix}
(m_1 + m_2)g - (m_1 + m_2)l_1\omega^2 & -m_2 l_2 \omega^2 \\
-m_2 l_1 \omega^2 & m_2 g - m_2 l_2 \omega^2
\end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix}
A_1 \\ A_2
\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}

For non-trivial solutions, the determinant of the coefficient matrix must be zero. Writing $x=\omega^2$, the characteristic equation becomes
\begin{equation}
m_1 l_1 l_2 x^2 - (m_1 + m_2)g(l_1 + l_2)x + (m_1 + m_2)g^2 = 0.
\end{equation}
Hence, for the general case with arbitrary masses and rod lengths, the squared angular frequencies are
\begin{equation}
\omega_{\pm}^2 =
\frac{g\left[(m_1 + m_2)(l_1 + l_2) \pm \sqrt{(m_1 + m_2)\left((m_1 + m_2)(l_1 + l_2)^2 - 4m_1 l_1 l_2\right)}\right]}{2m_1 l_1 l_2}.
\end{equation}
These expressions are the generalized small-angle angular frequencies obtained from the linearized form of the originally non-linear system. For simplicity, if we consider the special case where $m_1 = m_2 = m$ and $l_1 = l_2 = l$, the frequencies simplify to:
\begin{equation}
\omega_{\pm}^2 = \frac{g}{l} \left( 2 \pm \sqrt{2} \right).
\end{equation}
These two frequencies, $\omega_+$ and $\omega_-$, represent the two fundamental modes of vibration for the double pendulum.

\subsection{Physical Implications of Normal Modes}
The normal modes represent specific patterns of motion where all parts of the system oscillate with the same frequency and a fixed phase relation. 

For the in-phase mode, corresponding to the lower frequency $\omega_-^2 = (2 - \sqrt{2})\frac{g}{l}$, the amplitude ratio $A_2/A_1$ is positive (specifically, $\sqrt{2}$). Both pendulum masses swing in the same direction simultaneously. Because the lower string remains relatively aligned with the upper string, the overall restoring force from gravity is reduced. This naturally causes the mode to oscillate more slowly. What often gets overlooked, however, is the contrasting behaviour of the out-of-phase mode at the higher frequency $\omega_+^2 = (2 + \sqrt{2})\frac{g}{l}$. Here, the amplitude ratio is negative ($-\sqrt{2}$), meaning the two masses swing in opposite directions. This motion significantly bends the joint between the two rods, maximizing the restoring forces, which in turn leads to a much faster, higher-frequency oscillation. 

According to the superposition principle, any small-amplitude motion of the double pendulum can be described as a linear combination of these two fundamental normal modes. This theoretical foundation is crucial for verifying the accuracy of the numerical simulations presented in the following sections.

\subsection{Superposition State in the Linear Regime}
In the small-angle linear regime, the general solution is the superposition of the two normal modes:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\theta_1(t) &= c_- A_{1,-}\cos(\omega_- t + \phi_-) + c_+ A_{1,+}\cos(\omega_+ t + \phi_+), \\
\theta_2(t) &= c_- A_{2,-}\cos(\omega_- t + \phi_-) + c_+ A_{2,+}\cos(\omega_+ t + \phi_+),
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where $c_{\pm}$ are mode amplitudes set by initial conditions, $\phi_{\pm}$ are initial phases, and $(A_{1,\pm}, A_{2,\pm})$ are eigenvectors associated with each normal mode. This form shows that the observed trajectory is not usually a pure in-phase or out-of-phase oscillation, but a mixed state containing both. When both coefficients are non-zero, energy is distributed across modes and the measured motion may show modulation and phase-mixing effects. The superposition description remains accurate only while linear assumptions hold; for larger amplitudes, non-linear coupling progressively invalidates pure modal superposition and leads to strongly irregular motion.

\subsection{Linear Damping and the Rayleigh Dissipation Function}
Assuming the system experiences a linear air resistance proportional to velocity, the drag force on each mass $i$ is $\mathbf{F}_{di} = -c_i \mathbf{v}_i$, where $c_1$ and $c_2$ are the damping coefficients. To rigorously incorporate this dissipative force into the Lagrangian framework, we construct the Rayleigh dissipation function ($\mathcal{F}$), defined as half the rate of energy dissipation:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{F} = \frac{1}{2} c_1 \mathbf{v}_1^2 + \frac{1}{2} c_2 \mathbf{v}_2^2.
\end{equation}
Expanding the squared velocities and applying the small-angle approximation $\cos(\theta_1 - \theta_2) \approx 1$, the linearized dissipation function becomes:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{F} \approx \frac{1}{2}(c_1+c_2)l_1^2 \dot{\theta}_1^2 + \frac{1}{2}c_2 l_2^2 \dot{\theta}_2^2 + c_2 l_1 l_2 \dot{\theta}_1 \dot{\theta}_2.
\end{equation}
The generalized dissipative forces corresponding to the coordinates $\theta_1$ and $\theta_2$ are given by $Q_i = -\frac{\partial \mathcal{F}}{\partial \dot{\theta}_i}$.



\subsection{Characteristics of the Damped System}
The inclusion of the damping matrix $\mathbf{C}$ introduces several critical departures from the conservative, undamped system. Where the undamped model remains marginally stable with purely imaginary eigenvalues ($\lambda = \pm i\omega_n$) that dictate sustained harmonic oscillations, the damped system—assuming positive definite $\mathbf{M}, \mathbf{C}, \mathbf{K}$ and underdamped conditions—possesses complex eigenvalues with negative real parts ($\lambda = -\gamma \pm i\omega_d$). These negative real parts pull the solutions into the realm of exponentially decaying oscillations, $\theta_i(t) = A_i e^{-\gamma t} \cos(\omega_d t + \phi_0)$, rendering the system asymptotically stable. 

Equally telling is the subtle frequency drift that accompanies this stability. The very presence of damping slightly drags down the actual oscillation frequencies (the damped natural frequencies, $\omega_d$) compared to their undamped counterparts ($\omega_n$). A related, though mathematically more intricate, point concerns complex modes and coupling. In the idealized undamped scenario, the system exhibits ``real modes'': both masses pass through their equilibrium positions at the exact same instant, holding a strict phase difference of $0$ or $\pi$. The damping matrix $\mathbf{C}$, however, rarely diagonalizes via the same modal matrix that diagonalizes $\mathbf{M}$ and $\mathbf{K}$, unless proportional (Rayleigh) damping conditions are perfectly met. As a result, the system takes on ``complex modes.'' The motions of the two pendulums acquire a phase difference that is neither $0$ nor $\pi$. This physically manifests as a travelling wave characteristic rather than a pure standing wave, a much truer reflection of physical reality.

\section{Simulation and Data-Driven Analysis}

To investigate the underlying dynamics of the double pendulum system without relying heavily on prior theoretical formulations, a purely data-driven approach was adopted. Three sets of numerical simulations with varying system parameters (mass and length) were conducted using a high-precision symplectic integrator (Gauss-Legendre RK4). The initial conditions were set to a ``mixed-phase'' state ($\theta_1 = 0.1 \text{ rad}, \theta_2 = 0 \text{ rad}$) to simultaneously excite all possible intrinsic vibrational modes of the system.

\subsection{Empirical Analysis of the Simulated Motion}
Based on the graphical data presented in Figure \ref{fig:anti_phase_simulation}, we can extract critical physical insights about the double pendulum system purely from a data-driven perspective, even without prior knowledge of normal mode theory.

\subsubsection{Analysis of the Time-Domain Response (Top Graph)}
The top panel displays the angular displacement of both the upper (blue) and lower (orange) pendulums over a 20-second interval. The system starts at $t=0$ in an anti-phase configuration, with the upper pendulum displaced to $+0.10$ rad and the lower pendulum to $-0.10$ rad. The resulting motion is highly structured but far from a simple sine wave. We observe amplitude modulation, or beating, where the swings of both pendulums periodically grow and shrink. For instance, near $t=2.5$ s, the lower pendulum's swing is minimized while the upper pendulum swings widely; by $t=5$ s, the situation reverses. This squeezing effect indicates a continuous, periodic transfer of energy between the upper and lower segments. Despite this extreme beating phenomenon, the overall envelope repeats perfectly. This confirms the motion is a complex wave constructed from multiple pure rhythms rather than chaotic noise.

\subsubsection{Analysis of the Frequency-Domain Response (Middle Graph)}
The middle panel presents the Fourier spectrum of the upper pendulum's motion, transforming the complex time-domain signal into its fundamental frequency components. The spectrum reveals exactly two sharp, isolated peaks, proving that the complicated beating motion observed above is strictly the linear superposition of two simple, pure vibrations occurring simultaneously. A lower-frequency peak sits at $0.380 \text{ Hz}$, while a significantly taller higher-frequency peak is located at $0.920 \text{ Hz}$. In this specific anti-phase release, the faster $0.920 \text{ Hz}$ rhythm dominates the overall motion. The flat baseline further confirms that the small-angle motion is entirely deterministic and free of chaotic broadband frequencies.

\subsubsection{Simulation Restrictions and Formula Verification (Bottom Graph)}
While the simulation provides clean data, several physical and numerical restrictions must be acknowledged. The bottom graph shows a perfectly horizontal total mechanical energy line, drifting only by roughly $10^{-9}$ J. Real pendulums, of course, experience air resistance and pivot friction, meaning actual motion would inevitably decay over time. Furthermore, the simulation assumes $\sin\theta \approx \theta$. This approximation remains valid only for small displacements—here roughly $0.15$ rad, or $8.5^\circ$. For larger initial angles, the Fourier spectrum would inevitably exhibit harmonic distortions and, eventually, chaotic broadband noise.

To verify if the analytical theory correctly predicts the simulated data, we calculate the expected frequencies for a uniform double pendulum ($M_1=M_2=1.0\text{ kg}, L_1=L_2=1.0\text{ m}, g=9.8\text{ m/s}^2$) using the derived formula:
\begin{equation}
\omega_{\pm}^2 = \frac{g}{L}(2 \pm \sqrt{2})
\end{equation}
Converting angular frequency $\omega$ to ordinary frequency $f = \omega/(2\pi)$:
\begin{align}
    \omega_- &= \sqrt{9.8 \times (2 - 1.414)} \approx 2.396 \text{ rad/s} \implies f_{low} = \frac{2.396}{2\pi} \approx 0.381 \text{ Hz} \\
    \omega_+ &= \sqrt{9.8 \times (2 + 1.414)} \approx 5.784 \text{ rad/s} \implies f_{high} = \frac{5.784}{2\pi} \approx 0.920 \text{ Hz}
\end{align}
The theoretically predicted frequencies (0.381 Hz and 0.920 Hz) perfectly match the empirical peaks extracted via the Fast Fourier Transform (0.380 Hz and 0.920 Hz), validating both the linear analytical model and the numerical simulation pipeline.

This holds true only for small displacements—approximately $0.141$ rad, or $8.1^\circ$, in this case. Pushing the initial angles larger would inevitably introduce harmonic distortions into the Fourier spectrum, eventually giving way to chaotic broadband noise.

To verify if the analytical theory correctly predicts the simulated data, we calculate the expected frequencies for a uniform double pendulum ($M_1=M_2=1.0\text{ kg}, L_1=L_2=1.0\text{ m}, g=9.8\text{ m/s}^2$) using the derived formula:
\begin{equation}
\omega_{\pm}^2 = \frac{g}{L}(2 \pm \sqrt{2})
\end{equation}
Converting angular frequency $\omega$ to ordinary frequency $f = \omega/(2\pi)$:
\begin{align}
    \omega_- &= \sqrt{9.8 \times (2 - 1.414)} \approx 2.396 \text{ rad/s} \implies f_{low} = \frac{2.396}{2\pi} \approx 0.381 \text{ Hz} \\
    \omega_+ &= \sqrt{9.8 \times (2 + 1.414)} \approx 5.784 \text{ rad/s} \implies f_{high} = \frac{5.784}{2\pi} \approx 0.920 \text{ Hz}
\end{align}
The theoretically predicted frequencies (0.381 Hz and 0.920 Hz) perfectly match the empirical peaks extracted via the Fast Fourier Transform (0.380 Hz and 0.920 Hz), validating both the linear analytical model and the numerical simulation pipeline.

To understand how the mass distribution between the upper and lower pendulums affects the system's dynamics, we conducted a parameter sweep by varying the mass ratio $m_2/m_1$ from 0.1 to 5.0.

\subsubsection{Theoretical Frequency Shift}

    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{../../simulation_results/frequency_vs_mass_ratio.png}
    \caption{Theoretical normal mode frequencies ($f_{low}$ and $f_{high}$) as a function of the mass ratio $m_2/m_1$.}

As shown in Figure \ref{fig:frequency_mass_ratio}, altering the mass ratio significantly impacts the two fundamental frequencies. When the lower mass is very light ($m_2/m_1 \to 0$), the upper pendulum behaves almost like a simple pendulum, while the lower pendulum acts as a rapidly oscillating "whip" attached to it, leading to a massive divergence between the two frequencies. As the lower mass becomes heavier ($m_2/m_1$ increases), the in-phase frequency ($f_{low}$) steadily drops, and the anti-phase frequency ($f_{high}$) drops even more dramatically, causing the two frequencies to converge toward each other. This occurs because the massive lower bob essentially "anchors" the system, dragging down the overall oscillation pace.

Figure \ref{fig:energy_distribution} illustrates how kinetic energy is partitioned between the upper and lower segments during the two fundamental modes. Focusing first on the in-phase distribution shown in the left panel, the relationship between the independent variable—the mass ratio $\mu = m_2/m_1$ ranging from $0.1$ to $5.0$—and the normalized kinetic energy proportions ($E_{k1}$ and $E_{k2}$) becomes immediately apparent. The data naturally confirms the conservation relation $E_{k1} + E_{k2} = 1$ across all points, indicating no non-conservative energy loss in this parameter sweep. 

As $\mu$ increases, $E_{k1}$ monotonically decreases while $E_{k2}$ monotonically increases. What often gets overlooked, however, is the dramatic rate of this change in the small mass ratio region ($0 < \mu < 1.5$), which then becomes remarkably gentle for larger mass ratios ($\mu > 2.5$). Asymptotically, $E_{k1}$ approaches a minimum value near zero, leaving $E_{k2}$ to approach $1$. Although the curve visually resembles an exponential decay of the form $E_{k1}(\mu) = A e^{-k\mu} + C$, this is merely an empirical approximation rather than a strict analytical derivation. The horizontal asymptote $C \approx 0.04$ represents the residual kinetic energy of the upper pendulum, while the steep slope $k$ captures the system's extreme sensitivity to mass addition in the light-lower-bob regime. In reality, the exact mathematical form is an inverse rational function derived directly from the eigenvector amplitude ratios of the mass and stiffness matrices.

Physically, this kinetic energy proportion is dictated by the amplitude ratio of the normal modes. As the lower mass increases, the dynamic coupling strength changes non-linearly. The heavy lower bob essentially restricts the swing of the upper pendulum, forcing the vibrational energy to transfer downward into the lower segment. This leads to a critical insight for experimental guidance: the system's energy naturally biases toward the terminal segment. Even in the symmetric case ($\mu = 1$), the lower pendulum commands approximately 80\% of the kinetic energy in the in-phase mode. The highly sensitive region ($0.5 < \mu < 2.0$) thus provides the most effective range for tuning the mass to observe significant shifts in energy distribution. To observe prominent upper pendulum motion ($E_{k1} > 30\%$), the experimental design must utilize a significantly lighter lower bob ($\mu < 0.5$).

Conversely, in the fast, anti-phase mode (right panel), the energy distribution is highly sensitive to the mass ratio in a completely different manner. For a light lower bob ($m_2/m_1 < 1$), the upper pendulum dominates the kinetic energy. But as $m_2$ becomes heavier, the energy rapidly transfers. For large mass ratios, the lower pendulum ultimately dominates the kinetic energy in both modes.

\subsection{Phase Space Dynamics under Viscous Damping}

Visualizing the system’s trajectory in phase space offers a visceral understanding of its energy dissipation. What often gets overlooked, however, is how the initial modal excitation dictates the specific geometry of this collapse. In a mixed-phase release, where the upper pendulum is displaced while the lower hangs vertically, both normal modes are excited almost equally. The resulting phase portraits are highly complex, featuring tangled, overlapping loops rather than simple spirals. The upper pendulum begins at a clear displacement but immediately plunges into a wide, asymmetric arc, wandering erratically through phase space as energy sloshes violently into the lower segment. But this clean mathematical description barely captures the visual chaos of the crossing trajectories. Equally telling is the behavior of the lower pendulum, which starts entirely at rest in the center of its phase space. Rather than staying near equilibrium, it is violently whipped outward by the upper mass, carving out a massive, butterfly-like double lobe before it even begins to settle. This naturally leads to the question of how the energy dissipates during such violent excursions. In practice, of course, the reality is messier when the two modes are forced to compete for the remaining mechanical energy.

A related, though less discussed, point concerns the energy decay curve itself in relation to these phase plots. The steepest, most dramatic drops in total mechanical energy correspond exactly to the outermost, highest-velocity arcs of the phase trajectories. Beyond this, the non-conservative viscous forces are most punitive here, draining the system’s lifeblood precisely when it swings fastest, a detail easily missed if one only looks at the time-domain angles.

Ultimately, the persistence of this inward spiraling, despite the severe transient entanglement, confirms the presence of a strong global attractor. It transforms what would be a perpetual, quasi-periodic interplay of conservative modes into a deterministic, dissipative state.

The temporal evolution of the in-phase double pendulum system reveals a pronounced dampening effect, as evidenced by the rapid attenuation of both angular displacements. Both the upper ($\theta_1$) and lower ($\theta_2$) pendulums, initially displaced to 0.1 radians, oscillate synchronously before converging toward equilibrium within the first forty seconds of simulation. A related, though less discussed, point concerns the system's spectral signature. The Fourier spectrum of the upper pendulum isolates two distinct resonant frequencies, with a dominant peak at 0.379 Hz and a secondary, diminished peak at 0.920 Hz. This bifurcation hints at the underlying normal modes that persist even under non-conservative conditions.

Equally telling is the geometric trajectory mapped in the phase portraits. The inward spiraling trajectories for both the upper and lower masses trace a classic path toward a stable fixed point, starting from their initial displacements and ending precisely at the origin. But the rate at which this convergence occurs underscores the non-ideal nature of the physical model. What often gets overlooked, however, is the direct correlation between this kinematic decay and the system's energetics. The mechanical energy plot mirrors the phase-space collapse, showing a steep initial dissipation from approximately -29.25 J before stabilizing near -29.40 J after merely twenty seconds, as one might anticipate in a highly damped environment.

Ultimately, the persistence of this rapid energy loss and modal decay suggests a significant dissipative mechanism at play, dominating the transient dynamics. In practice, of course, the reality is messier. The system's rapid settling into its final quiescent state reflects a strong damping coefficient, a factor that fundamentally reshapes the expected conservative behavior into a straightforward decay to rest.

\section{Conclusion}
The weight of the evidence points toward a profound interplay between linear predictability and non-linear complexity within the double pendulum system. While the small-angle approximation successfully captures the fundamental modal frequencies, pushing the system even slightly beyond this regime inevitably invokes Coriolis and centrifugal effects that dictate a continuous, periodic transfer of energy between the upper and lower segments. Ultimately, the persistence of this dynamic coupling suggests that analytical mechanics and numerical simulation must be deployed in tandem; one provides the baseline, while the other maps the chaotic frontier. Future investigations would benefit immensely from incorporating real-time feedback control, a step that would transform this theoretical model into a testbed for active stabilization strategies.
